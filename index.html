01 — The Basics

What is a Quadratic?

A quadratic equation is any polynomial equation of degree 2. The standard form is ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0. If a were 0, it'd just be a boring linear equation — no drama, no roots worth writing home about.

The graph of a quadratic is a parabola. It opens upward when a > 0 (happy face 🙂) and downward when a < 0 (sad face 🙁). Where the parabola crosses the x-axis? Those are your roots.

f(x) = ax² + bx + c a, b, c ∈ ℝ, a ≠ 0 — the three coefficients that define everything

02 — The Formula

The Quadratic Formula

Derived by completing the square on the standard form. This single formula gives you both roots for any quadratic — no guessing, no factoring struggles.

x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a The ± gives you two roots: x₁ with + and x₂ with −

The term inside the square root, b² − 4ac, is the discriminant (Δ). It's the oracle — tells you everything about the nature of roots before you even solve.

03 — The Discriminant

Reading the Discriminant (Δ = b² − 4ac)

Condition Nature of Roots What it means
Δ > 0 Two distinct real roots Parabola crosses x-axis at 2 points
Δ = 0 One repeated real root Parabola just touches the x-axis (tangent)
Δ < 0 No real roots (complex pair) Parabola floats above/below x-axis entirely
Δ > 0, perfect square Two rational roots Factorable over integers — easy mode

04 — Vieta's Formulas

Sum & Product of Roots

If α and β are the two roots, Vieta's formulas let you relate them to the coefficients without solving the equation. Insanely useful for JEE problems.

α + β = −b/a Sum of roots
α · β = c/a Product of roots

So if you need to form a quadratic with given roots α and β, just write: x² − (α+β)x + αβ = 0. Done.

05 — Methods

Ways to Solve a Quadratic

1

Factorization

Split the middle term. Works cleanly when roots are rational integers. Fast if you spot it.

2

Completing the Square

Convert to (x + p)² = q form. The method behind the formula itself. Good for derivations.

3

Quadratic Formula

The nuclear option. Always works, no exceptions. Plug a, b, c → get roots. That's it.

4

Graphical Method

Plot f(x) = ax² + bx + c. Where it touches the x-axis are your roots. Great for intuition.
